Press Freedom and Donald Trump: A Doomed Combination

By Amanda Sinofsky

Freedom of the press is a right enshrined in the First Amendment of the Constitution. However, it has been challenged time and time again in the United States. Free press has been under threat for years, and President Trump is at the forefront. Trump’s war on the news media has free press in jeopardy.

The Background

In 2025, the United States ranked #57 out of 180 countries in the Reporters Without Borders' World Press Freedom Index. This is a two place decrease from its rank of #55 in 2024, and twelve places below its rank of #45 in 2023. This is the lowest ranking the U.S. has received since appearing in the index. United States first appeared in the index in 2013. A country’s ranking in the index is determined by the “ability of journalists as individuals and collectives to select, produce, and disseminate news in the public interest independent of political, economic, legal, and social interference and in the absence of threats to their physical and mental safety,” according to RSF.

While the United States has consistently had a satisfactory amount of press freedom, especially in comparison to the rest of the world, this is not to say that journalists are not experiencing hardships and barriers for simply doing their jobs.

White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt answers questions from the news media in the White House briefing room on April 8, 2025. (Leah Millis/REUTERS)

The Politics

According to a survey conducted by the Pew Research Center, about seven-in-ten Americans are at least somewhat concerned about press freedom in 2025, with about 4-in-ten Americans extremely/very concerned. These numbers have not changed much in the past year, but the demographic of citizens that make them up has.

In the past year, Democrats have become more concerned about press freedom than Republicans. In 2024, 47% of Republicans and right-leaning Americans were concerned about press freedoms, while only 38% of Democrats and left-leaning Americans were. In 2025, 60% of Democrats are concerned about press freedoms, while only 28% of Republicans are. This change came along with the shift from the Biden Administration to the Trump Administration from 2024 to 2025.

Along with that, in 2024, Democrats were more likely than Republicans to believe that the media was completely free to report the news. In 2025, Republicans are more likely to say this than Democrats.

The President

In 2017, during President Trump’s first term, he called the news media an "enemy of the American people.” In his second term, President Trump has stuck to that sentiment.

The FAKE NEWS media (failing @nytimes, @NBCNews, @ABC, @CBS, @CNN) is not my enemy, it is the enemy of the American People!

— Donald J. Trump (@realDonaldTrump) February 17, 2017

In the past six months, the U.S. Press Freedom Tracker has recorded six “chilling statements” related to the Trump Administration. The U.S. Press Freedom Tracker defines chilling statements as “select public threats made to reporters and media organizations by U.S. politicians and other public figures.” One of the most recent targets of these threats is the Associated Press.

The Associated Press logo is shown at the news organization’s office in New York on July 13, 2023. (AP Photo/Aaron Jackson)

As of Apr. 30, 2025, AP has been barred from the White House for 78 days. On Feb. 11, the White House banned AP for refusing to acknowledge the name change of the Gulf of Mexico to the Gulf of America. Despite a court order that ruled the government cannot bar journalists from the White House based on their viewpoints, on Apr. 14, an AP reporter and photographer were barred from an Oval Office news conference.

The Future

Judging by the course of President Trump’s first 100 days of his second term, he is unlikely to end his war on the press any time soon. The U.S. Press Freedom Tracker has recorded 23 incidents of journalists being targeted so far in 2025, and this number will only grow.

According to the Reporter’s Committee for the Freedom of the Press, the best way to support press freedom is to stay informed and demand government transparency. Today’s political climate may need that now more than ever.
